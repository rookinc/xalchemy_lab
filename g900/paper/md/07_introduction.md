# Introduction

## Status
Working draft

The present paper studies a localized obstruction phenomenon in the G900 witness machine.

The starting point is a failure mode already exposed by the baseline two-step policy: on the sampled run, that policy is monotone in normalized action distance, so it contracts toward exactness, but it produces no nontrivial exact closure. In slogan form, the machine exhibits contraction without closure.

That initial failure, however, does not by itself justify a deep obstruction claim. A substantial part of the failure could still be due to policy selection rather than the underlying witness-machine geometry. For that reason, the first major step in the present program was to introduce an exact-preference repair. Computationally, that repair succeeds where it most needs to succeed: it restores the dominant reachable frame-0 chamber. This changes the meaning of the remaining failure. After repair, what survives is no longer best described as a global inability to organize toward exactness. Instead, the residual basin sharpens and localizes.

The key observation is that, after exact-preference repair, the remaining unresolved flow funnels into the frame-2 near-action seam. More strongly, the residual mismatch localizes to a single normalized coordinate: slot 4. In the repaired regime, exact frame-2 closure requires the slot-4 value \(t2\). Thus the question ceases to be an undifferentiated reachability problem on the full machine and becomes a sharply localized installation problem at one seam coordinate.

This localization makes it possible to define a bounded theorem program. Starting from a distinguished locked subset of frame-2-nearest witnesses, one considers the bounded escape-return regime generated by legal one-edit moves subject to the action-distance cutoff \(d_A \le 3\). That regime saturates computationally by depth 6, with no new states appearing at depths 7 or 8. The total number of visited states is 58,624. Within this saturated bounded regime, no exact frame-2 closure appears. More importantly, the exact slot-4 value \(t2\) never appears anywhere in the regime.

This nonappearance is the main obstruction signal. It is stronger than a bare no-closure statement, because \(t2\) is not merely another symbol that happened not to occur: it is the exact slot value required for normalized frame-2 closure. The current bounded result therefore supports a theorem-shaped claim: within the bounded escape-return regime generated from the locked frame-2 witnesses, the exact slot-4 installation value is excluded.

A second structural feature strengthens this reading. On the frame-2 seam itself, the observed slot-4 alphabet is not arbitrary; it is
\[
\{o4,s0,s2,s3,s4,t0,t3,t4\}.
\]
Thus the repaired machine reaches the seam and circulates among nearby seam-compatible slot-4 values, but it does not install the exact value required to seal the seam. This suggests that the residual obstruction is not a global loss of orientation, but a local transport failure or defect-preservation law.

The aim of the present paper is therefore twofold. The first aim is to record the bounded obstruction cleanly and conservatively as a mathematically precise computational statement. The second aim is to formulate the proof program that could convert this computational result into a structural theorem. The most promising route is to derive a projected slot-4 transport relation from the one-edit grammar and the normalization law, prove that the reachable seam alphabet is closed under that projected transport, and then show that \(t2\) lies outside the resulting closed alphabet.

Several candidate explanations are currently under consideration. One possibility is a direct grammar-induced admissibility restriction at slot 4 near the frame-2 seam. Another is an affine residue law, likely modulo 5, preserved by bounded legal one-edits after normalization. A third is that the observed seam alphabet forms a normalization orbit or orbit slice that does not contain \(t2\). A fourth, conceptually strongest, possibility is that the slot-4 mismatch is a localized installation defect that bounded one-edits can transport but not annihilate.

The paper does not yet claim a global impossibility theorem. It makes a bounded claim, explicitly tied to the normalized witness machine and to the bounded regime generated from the locked frame-2 witnesses. This restraint is deliberate. The computational result is already precise and nontrivial, and the correct next step is not indiscriminate search expansion but structural explanation.

The central theorem target may therefore be stated informally as follows:

> after chamber-level repair, the residual frame-2 obstruction becomes a one-slot exclusion law.

Or, in the shorter working slogan that captures the geometry:

> the machine finds the seam without sealing it.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 fixes the formal objects. Section 3 records the computational ladder leading from baseline contraction failure to the bounded frame-2 regime. Section 4 states the bounded slot-4 exclusion result. Section 5 develops the structural proof program via projected slot-4 transport. Section 6 discusses candidate invariant mechanisms. Section 7 addresses scope and referee-style objections. Section 8 lists the main open problems.
