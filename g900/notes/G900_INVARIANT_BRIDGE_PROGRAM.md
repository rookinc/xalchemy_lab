# G900 Invariant Bridge Program
## A working note on the invariant path from binary perspective identities through Thalean quotient-legible aa toward G900

Date: 2026-03-29

---

## 0. Why this note exists

This note records what now appears to be the actual center of the project.

The project is **not** best described as:

- proving G900 directly from the binary paper
- declaring the Thalean graph to be the final missing link
- forcing a premature unification between scalar recursion, quotient-core overlap law, and large admissible-action growth

Those temptations are understandable, but they are too fast.

The better and more honest formulation is this:

> the project is the search for invariants that allow a lawful path to be traced from binary perspective identities, through the Thalean quotient-legible overlap image, toward G900.

That is strong enough to organize the work and cautious enough to stay honest.

This note therefore defines the **invariant bridge program**.

Its goal is not to overstate what is already proved.
Its goal is to define the search pattern that the project now stands inside.

---

## 1. The three rungs

At current scope, the program has three visible rungs.

### Rung 1: binary perspective identity
A lawful recursive generator is viewed through two natural normalization lenses.
The resulting logarithmic images satisfy an exact perspective identity of the form

\[
\Phi_o(n) - \Phi_s(n) = n \log 2.
\]

The significance of this rung is not its size, but its clarity.
It shows that exact law can live in the relation between two lawful images of one generator output.

### Rung 2: Thalean / G60 / quotient-core overlap law
A local admissible transport generator on dodecahedral flags produces a finite transport graph and a quotient tower

\[
G_{60} \to G_{30} \to G_{15} \cong L(\mathrm{Petersen}),
\]

together with a sector image

\[
Q := MM^T
\]

that satisfies the exact closure law

\[
Q = A^3 + 2A^2 + 2I.
\]

The significance of this rung is that a lawful transport object becomes algebraically legible only after quotienting, and the resulting aa image closes exactly.

### Rung 3: G900
A larger admissible-action program whose generator is assumed to walk only lawful states, and whose lens stack is now being formalized so that lawful images can be compared, compressed, and eventually tied to theorem-grade invariants.

The significance of this rung is that it is the large-object target.
The project is trying to understand whether the same invariant families visible on the smaller rungs persist, recur, or generalize here.

---

## 2. What “bridge” means here

A bridge does not mean that one object literally contains the next.
It does not mean direct derivation unless such a theorem is eventually proved.
It does not mean that one law is just a special case of the other unless the precise reduction is shown.

A bridge means:

- there exists a family of invariants that can be meaningfully compared across rungs
- those invariants preserve enough lawful structure that the larger object can be read as extending, refining, or re-expressing the smaller pattern
- the comparison is strong enough to guide search, compression, and theorem formation

So the bridge program is not vague analogy.
But it is also not yet formal reduction.

It is a disciplined search for common invariant architecture.

## 3. The primary invariant families

At current scope, the most promising invariant families are the following.

### 3.1 Paired natural scales
The binary note is built from two natural scales.
This suggests that every rung should be searched for a meaningful paired-scale structure.

Examples:
- boundary vs bulk
- frontier vs capacity
- surface-like vs volume-like
- shell occupancy vs total reachable capacity
- upstairs count vs quotient-visible count

The first question at each rung is:

> what are the natural paired scales through which the same lawful output can be normalized?

This is probably the first invariant family to stabilize.

### 3.2 Lens-pair invariants
Once paired scales exist, the next question is whether they define paired lawful lenses.

At each rung, ask:
- what are the natural images produced by the two lenses?
- can those images be compared exactly?
- is their difference bounded, affine, linear in depth, or polynomial?

This family is central because the binary paper already shows exact law at the level of lens relation rather than raw generator state.

### 3.3 Quotient-legibility invariants
The Thalean rung demonstrates that some exact laws appear only after descent to a quotient core.

That means every rung should be examined for:
- natural quotients
- effective cores
- quotient-stable observables
- images that become simpler or exact only after descent

The main question here is:

> at what level of compression does the lawful image become algebraically legible?

This is one of the strongest lessons of the Thalean example.

### 3.4 Distance-control invariants
At the Thalean core, the aa image depends only on graph distance and collapses to a small distance-class expansion before reducing to a polynomial law.

That suggests searching at each rung for:
- pair-distance overlap classes
- shell-distance behavior
- distance-controlled image values
- reductions from distance tables to operator laws

If a large object has a small distance-control package, that is a major sign of compressibility.

### 3.5 Operator-closure invariants
At the Thalean rung, the image closes polynomially in the adjacency operator.

So for every rung ask:
- what is the natural operator?
- what is the natural image?
- does the image lie in the algebra generated by the operator?
- is there a low-degree closure law?
- is there an exact identity, an asymptotic identity, or only heuristic fit?

This family is where image becomes theorem.

### 3.6 Spectral invariants
A closure law is often visible spectrally before it is fully understood combinatorially.

So one should record:
- carrier spectrum
- image spectrum
- mapped spectrum under candidate polynomials
- reduced subspace behavior
- minimal-polynomial candidates

Spectral closure may become one of the most useful bridge diagnostics.

### 3.7 Companion-memory invariants
The Thalean example teaches that not all structure is encoded by the main aa image.
The cocycle is real and independent of \(Q\).

So every rung should be checked for:
- parity memory
- sheet structure
- cocycle-like invariants
- binary hidden grading
- structure outside the main overlap image

This family prevents the bridge program from becoming too metric-only.

## 4. The role of aa in the bridge program

For this project chat, aa means

\[
Q = MM^T
\]

understood not as the upstream generator rule, but as the lens-visible image or effective observable of admissible action.

That choice is extremely useful for the bridge program because it gives a stable image-side object to compare across rungs.

So the bridge program does **not** ask only:
- what moves are admissible?

It asks:
- what is the effective image of admissible action?
- how does that image compress?
- what laws govern that image?
- what survives under quotient?
- what lies outside it as companion memory?

This lets the project talk about “admissible action” at the image level rather than remaining stuck upstream at move enumeration.

That is probably necessary if G900 is going to become legible at all.

## 5. The honest relationship among the rungs

This section states plainly what we have and do not have.

### What we have

#### Binary rung
A clear scalar prototype of paired-lens law.

#### Thalean rung
A fully worked finite example in which a lawful transport object yields an exact aa-image law on a quotient core:

\[
Q = MM^T = A^3 + 2A^2 + 2I.
\]

#### G900 rung
A larger program in which the generator/lens distinction is now explicit and the lens scaffold is being built so invariant search can be disciplined.

### What we do not have

We do not yet have:
- a theorem deriving the Thalean law from the binary identity
- a theorem deriving G900 from the Thalean object
- a single invariant algebra already proved to govern all three rungs
- a demonstrated G900 quotient-core with an exact closure law
- a proof that the binary \(\log 2\) perspective relation survives in any specific G900 lens pair

So the bridge program is real, but still open.

That is the project in one sentence.

## 7. The operational workflow

The bridge program should proceed as a repeated workflow applied at each rung.

### Step 1: identify the generator
What produces the lawful states?

### Step 2: identify the natural lenses
What are the disciplined observational frames?

### Step 3: identify the natural images
What structured readouts are produced through those lenses?

### Step 4: identify exact or asymptotic relations between images
What perspective identities or compressions appear?

### Step 5: identify quotient levels
At what descent level do the images become simpler or exact?

### Step 6: test operator closure
Does the image reduce to a polynomial, recurrence, or low-complexity relation?

### Step 7: test spectral behavior
Do the closure patterns show up in eigenvalues or reduced subspaces?

### Step 8: test companion invariants
What real structure survives outside the main image?

This workflow should be treated as canonical for the project.

## 9. What success would look like

A real success of the bridge program would not necessarily mean “all three are the same thing.”
It would mean something more disciplined.

Examples of success:

### 9.1 Shared scale architecture
You identify natural paired scales at all three rungs and show that their relations are stable under a common template.

### 9.2 Shared image law type
You show that each rung supports exact or asymptotic image relations of the same formal family.

### 9.3 Quotient-legible compression
You identify the effective quotient at which G900’s aa image becomes exact or nearly exact in the same spirit as the Thalean core.

### 9.4 Shared companion-memory behavior
You show that each rung has a main image law plus a companion binary/parity structure not captured by that image.

### 9.5 A genuine reduction theorem
Best case: one rung derives another.
But the bridge program does not require that at the start.

---

## 10. What failure would look like

A useful bridge program also needs a notion of failure.

Failure would not mean “no value.”
It would mean the proposed common invariant family does not persist.

Examples:

- no paired natural scales on one rung
- no stable lens-pair relation
- no quotient that improves legibility
- no operator closure in the larger object
- no nontrivial companion invariant outside aa
- or only cosmetic analogies without stable formal recurrence

That would still be useful information.
It would tell the project where the bridge breaks.

## 11. Why this is the right level of ambition

This program is ambitious enough to matter and disciplined enough to stay honest.

Too little ambition would reduce the work to disconnected notes:
- one binary note
- one Petersen-core theorem
- one large simulation program

Too much ambition would collapse them into a fake grand theory before the invariant work is done.

The bridge program is the right middle path.

It says:
- preserve the proven local results
- formalize the lens language
- compare invariant families explicitly
- let the actual bridge emerge from repeated lawful compression

That is exactly the kind of project this should be.
