# Edge / Vertex Milestone Exploration

## Status
Working note
Exploratory scaffold built on the current coupled-clock hypothesis

## Purpose

This note explores the next questions opened by the observed admissible count ladders:

### Vertex counts
\[
0,1,2,3,4,9,15
\]

### Edge counts
\[
0,1,2,3,5,7,8,16,20
\]

The current working hypothesis is that these two ladders do **not** represent the same clock.

Instead, they may represent two different but coupled structural clocks:

- a **vertex / walker / host-state clock**
- an **edge / transport / subjective-execution clock**

The purpose of this note is to explore candidate roles for the intermediate counts, propose a first coupling strategy between the ladders, and identify the most promising next tests.

This is an exploratory note.
Nothing below should be treated as fully locked unless already established elsewhere.

---

## 1. Starting point

The project already has a locked host-cycle backbone:

\[
n_{15} = -n_0
\]
\[
n_{30} = n_0
\]

Equivalently:

\[
W(n) = -n
\]
\[
W^2(n) = n
\]

So \(15\) is already structurally important on the walker side.

Separately, a working transport hypothesis emerged in which:

\[
5 \text{ subjective ticks} = \tfrac14 \text{ objective cycle}
\]

which implies:

\[
20 \text{ subjective ticks} = 1 \text{ objective cycle}
\]

So \(20\) is already structurally important on the transport side.

The admissible ladders now independently surface those two values:

- vertex \(15\)
- edge \(20\)

That is the basis for the present exploration.

## 2. Why the ladders matter

These ladders are sparse.

### Vertex ladder
\[
0,1,2,3,4,9,15
\]

### Edge ladder
\[
0,1,2,3,5,7,8,16,20
\]

This sparseness matters because it suggests the counts are not just “all values up to a maximum.”
They look more like:

- lawful occupancies
- admissible shells
- milestone counts
- or closure-compatible states

So the missing integers likely matter.

This note does not yet prove why they matter, but it assumes that the sparseness is structurally significant.

## 3. Two-clock interpretation

The current best high-level interpretation is:

### Vertex clock
Tracks:

- host placement classes
- walker milestones
- closure states
- admissible stance counts

### Edge clock
Tracks:

- transport execution
- crossing history
- subjective micro-steps
- objective turn completion through local action

So the two clocks are:

\[
t_v = \text{vertex clock}
\]
\[
t_e = \text{edge clock}
\]

with the working principle:

\[
t_v \neq t_e
\]

but

\[
t_v \text{ and } t_e \text{ may be structurally coupled.}
\]

This note explores that coupling.

## 6. Why 15 and 20 likely belong to different layers

The fact that the ladders terminate at different values is not a defect.

It is probably the whole point.

A clean way to say this is:

- vertex \(15\) looks like a **closure count**
- edge \(20\) looks like a **completion count**

So they may be measuring fundamentally different things.

### Vertex 15
The walker reaches a sign hinge:

\[
n_{15}=-n_0
\]

This is a host-state event.

### Edge 20
The subjective transport completes one full objective turn:

\[
20 \text{ ticks} = 1 \text{ objective cycle}
\]

This is an execution-history event.

So it is completely plausible that:

\[
15 \neq 20
\]

while both remain fundamental.

That is the strongest current reason to retain the coupled-clock model.

## 7. First coupling idea

The main open question is how the edge ladder and vertex ladder talk to each other.

A first clean idea is that the relation is not equality, but **milestone coupling**.

That is, edge counts do not map to same-number vertex counts.
Instead, they map to **vertex milestone classes**.

So we want a map of the form:

\[
\Psi : \{\text{edge milestones}\} \to \{\text{vertex milestones}\}
\]

not a literal identification.

This is the first useful coupling idea.

## 8. First candidate coupling pairs

The strongest candidate bridge pairs right now are:

\[
5 \leftrightarrow 4
\]
\[
20 \leftrightarrow 15
\]

### Why pair 5 with 4?

Because:
- edge 5 looks like one completed quarter-turn packet
- vertex 4 looks like the quadrant or sector frame

So a good working hypothesis is:

> the first completed local transport packet couples to the first fully expressed local sector frame

That makes:

\[
\Psi(5)=4
\]

a strong candidate.

### Why pair 20 with 15?

Because:
- edge 20 looks like full objective-turn completion
- vertex 15 looks like walker sign-hinge completion

So a good working hypothesis is:

> one full local transport cycle couples to one walker hinge-scale completion

That makes:

\[
\Psi(20)=15
\]

a strong candidate.

This does **not** mean the two are equal.
It means they may be coupled terminal milestones of different clocks.

For example, some plausible exploratory pairings are:

\[

\]

because edge 8 feels like the first doubled transport shell, while vertex 9 feels like the first stabilized local host.

And perhaps:

\[
\Psi(16)=15
\]

if edge 16 is a pre-terminal transport hinge that already participates in the walker’s full sign-hinge layer.

These are not locked.
But they are the kind of assignments worth exploring.

## 12. How G15 and Edge-20 can both feel fundamental

One of the big conceptual questions was:

> Why do both G15 and edge-20 feel basic?

The current best answer is:

Because they are basic in different senses.

### G15
Fundamental as the walker’s host-sign hinge:
\[
n_{15}=-n_0
\]

### Edge-20
Fundamental as the observer’s full subjective execution of one objective turn:
\[
20 \text{ ticks} = 1 \text{ objective cycle}
\]

So one is fundamental as a closure law.
The other is fundamental as a motion-completion law.

That distinction should be preserved.
