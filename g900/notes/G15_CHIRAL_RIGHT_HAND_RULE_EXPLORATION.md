# G15 Chiral Right-Hand Rule Exploration

## Status
Working exploratory note

## Purpose

This note explores a modified transport law on the G15 host frame in which a right-hand 90-degree turn is injected at every local event.

The goal is not to preserve the original G15/G30 cycle by assumption.
The goal is to test whether systematic right-handed bias reveals a distinct chiral transport regime.

---

## Core idea

The neutral host walk supports the currently locked cycle law:

\[
n_{15} = -n_0
\]
\[
n_{30} = n_0
\]

This neutral law is the discovery-and-confirmation regime.

We now ask what happens if, instead of following only the neutral host continuation, we impose a compulsory right-hand quarter turn at every local event.

This defines a new transport law.

---

## 1. Neutral law versus chiral law

### Neutral law
The neutral law follows the host grammar without forcing a handed preference at every step.

It is currently interpreted as:

- first pass discovers the map
- second pass confirms the map and restores the original stance

So the neutral law is primarily epistemic and restorative.

### Chiral right-hand law
The modified law adds a right-hand 90-degree turn at each local event.

Informally:

> whenever the walker reaches a lawful local event, the walker turns right before continuing

This is not just a rotated copy of the old walk.
It is a new pathing law with explicit chirality.

---

## 2. Why this matters

The right-hand rule is a test of whether the host carries real handed structure.

If left and right are genuinely meaningful in the host, then a systematic right-turn rule should reveal something structural, not merely cosmetic.

So this law can be used as a chirality probe.

The central question is:

> Does the always-right rule generate a closed orbit, or does it create directed propagation through neighboring hosts?

---

## 3. Minimal symbolic form

Let:

- \(M\) = move / continue transport
- \(R\) = right-hand quarter turn

Then the right-biased local update can be represented schematically as:

\[
T_R = R \circ M
\]

or depending on convention,

\[
T_R = M \circ R
\]

The exact order must be fixed by the local pathing convention.
But in either case, the point is the same:

> every local continuation is modified by an injected right-hand turn

This makes \(T_R\) a chiral transport operator rather than the neutral walk operator.

---

## 4. Three likely behaviors

### Behavior A. Small chiral loop
The right-hand rule may collapse into a shorter local orbit.

For example, the walker may become trapped in a repeating face-loop, edge-loop, or corner-loop.

If this happens, the right-turn law reveals a local chiral attractor.

### Behavior B. Directed geodesic drift
The right-hand rule may fail to restore the original stance and instead propagate persistently through neighboring hosts.

If this happens, the right-turn law acts as a generator of directed extension.

This is currently the most exciting possibility.

### Behavior C. Mirror-paired chiral families
The right-hand rule may generate one lawful orbit family, while the always-left rule generates its mirror.

If so, the host decomposes into paired chiral transport classes.

This would strongly support the claim that the host carries real handed path structure.

---

## 5. Working hypothesis

The current best hypothesis is:

- the neutral G15/G30 law is the discovery / confirmation / restoration regime
- the always-right rule is not primarily restorative
- the always-right rule is likely a branch-selection or propagation rule

So the chiral rule may be better thought of as a generative law than as a closure law.

In compact form:

- neutral law = host comprehension
- chiral law = host emission or directed continuation

This distinction is not yet proven, but it is a strong working read.

---

## 6. Relation to G30

G30 is currently treated as the restored-identity hinge:

\[
n_{30} = n_0
\]

From this hinge, multiple relaunches are possible.

The right-hand rule is especially interesting when applied after G30, because the walker is then in a restored lawful stance.

So after G30, the right-hand chiral launch becomes a clean test of host anisotropy or host chirality.

A useful question is:

> starting from restored identity, does the always-right relaunch stay in the original host, orbit locally, or propagate into neighboring hosts?

This is the most immediate useful experiment.

---

## 7. Neutral, right, and left families

It is useful to distinguish three transport families.

### Neutral family
\[
W
\]

This is the currently locked discovery-confirmation family.

### Right-handed family
\[
W_R
\]

This is the always-right chiral family.

### Left-handed family
\[
W_L
\]

This is the always-left chiral family.

The next structural question is:

\[
W_R \stackrel{?}{=} W_L^{-1}
\]

or more weakly:

> Are the right and left laws mirror-related?

If they are, then the host supports paired chiral propagation.
If not, the host may have deeper asymmetry.

---

## 8. Interpretive distinction

The neutral walk seems to answer:

> what is the lawful map of the host?

The chiral right-hand walk seems to answer:

> what happens when lawful transport is forced to favor one side at every choice?

So the neutral law is descriptive.
The chiral law is selective.

That is why the chiral law may produce drift rather than return.

---

## 9. Practical exploration questions

The following questions should guide the next round of thought experiments.

1. Does the always-right law still close after 15 steps?
2. If not, does it close after some shorter or longer period?
3. Does it remain in one host frame, or propagate into a neighboring host?
4. Is the always-left law the mirror of the always-right law?
5. Does the chiral law create a geodesic of witness-linked states more directly than the neutral law does?

These are the main questions worth testing.

## 10. Best current guess

The best current guess is:

- the always-right rule does not preserve the neutral G15 closure law
- it likely generates a directed chiral continuation
- that continuation may be one branch of a larger witness-propagation structure
- the always-left rule may generate the mirror branch

So the host may admit:

- a neutral closure regime
- a right-handed propagation regime
- a left-handed propagation regime

If so, this would be one of the strongest distinctions in the model so far.
